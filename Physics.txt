•	kinematics: The branch of physics that describes the motion of objects without considering the forces that cause the motion
•	dynamics: The branch of physics concerned with the forces that cause motion and changes in motion
•	Newton's First Law: An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and direction unless acted upon by an unbalanced force
•	Newton's Second Law: The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass (F = ma)
•	Newton's Third Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction
•	momentum: The product of an object's mass and velocity (p = mv)
•	work: The energy transferred to or from an object via the application of force along a displacement (W = F·d)
•	energy: The capacity to do work; comes in forms such as kinetic, potential, thermal, etc.
•	power: The rate at which work is done or energy is transferred (P = W/t)
•	thermodynamics: The branch of physics that deals with heat, work, and temperature, and their relation to energy and entropy
•	entropy: A measure of the disorder or randomness in a system
•	quantum mechanics: The branch of physics that deals with the behavior of matter and light on the atomic and subatomic scale
•	wave-particle duality: The concept that every particle or quantum entity exhibits both wave and particle properties
•	electromagnetic spectrum: The range of all types of electromagnetic radiation, from radio waves to gamma rays
•	relativity: Einstein's theories describing the relationship between space, time, mass, and energy
•	nuclear fission: The splitting of a heavy atomic nucleus into lighter nuclei, releasing energy
•	nuclear fusion: The process by which two light atomic nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus, releasing energy
•	superconductivity: The phenomenon of exactly zero electrical resistance occurring in certain materials when cooled below a characteristic temperature
•	dark matter: A hypothetical form of matter that does not emit, absorb, or reflect light but accounts for most of the matter in the universe
•	Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle: The principle that states that the position and momentum of a particle cannot be simultaneously measured with arbitrarily high precision
